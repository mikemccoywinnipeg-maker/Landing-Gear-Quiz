📚 What "dynamic drives" covers

Everything that carries power from the engine to the rotors — in ATA chapter terms, mostly ATA 63 (main rotor drive) and ATA 65 (tail rotor drive):

  • Clutches, belt drive systems, and freewheeling (overrunning) units
  • Driveshafts and flexible couplings, engine-to-transmission and down the tailboom
  • The main gearbox: gear stages, mounts, mast, and torque limits
  • Lubrication systems, chip detectors, oil analysis, and health monitoring (HUMS)
  • Intermediate and tail rotor gearboxes, hanger bearings, rotor brakes
  • Vibration fundamentals and drivetrain troubleshooting
  • Inspections, conditional inspections (sudden stoppage, overtorque), life-limited parts, and the airworthiness picture

Not in scope (usually a separate rotors course, but confirm in Week 0): rotor heads, blade systems, main rotor track & balance, and flight controls. You'll want a clean boundary agreed with whoever teaches rotors.

Week 1 · Jul 27 – Aug 2

Module 1 — The Powertrain Big Picture

Before any component makes sense, you (and later your students) need the whole power path in your head.

🎓 Why this is your first lecture

Students who can trace power from the engine to both rotors, and explain why each element exists, learn every later component as "of course that's there." Students who can't are memorizing parts lists. This module rebuilds that map for you first.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Trace the complete power path of a typical single-rotor helicopter and name every element in order.
  • Explain why large speed reduction is unavoidable (tip speed, torque, P = τω).
  • Contrast piston and turbine drivetrains and say why one needs a clutch and the other doesn't.
  • Describe the drive consequences of tandem, coaxial, intermeshing, NOTAR, and Fenestron layouts.
  • Quote ballpark RPM and reduction numbers for common types, and find the exact ones in a maintenance manual.
  • Navigate ATA chapters 62–65 in a type MM and know what lives where.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

1.1  Why a drive system exists at all

A main rotor is a huge wing that must keep its advancing blade tip comfortably below the speed of sound (drag and vibration climb fast beyond roughly Mach 0.85–0.9 at the tip). Tip speed = rotor radius × rotational speed, so big rotors are slow rotors: real machines run roughly 200–530 main rotor RPM. Engines are the opposite: piston engines like to make power near 2,500–3,000 RPM, and free-turbine output shafts spin at 6,000–21,000 RPM. Something has to bridge that gap.

The second half of the story is torque. Power = torque × rotational speed (P = τω). Cut the speed by a factor of 15 at constant power and the torque coming out is 15× the torque going in. That is why the final gear stage, the mast, and the mounts are the beefiest parts of the drivetrain, and why the gearbox — not the engine — often sets the aircraft's power limit.

Classroom hook Ask: "The engine makes 6,000 RPM. The rotor needs 400. Who votes we just bolt them together?" Then let the class reason their way to tip speed and torque. Ten minutes, no slides, and they've derived the reason the course exists.
1.2  The standard power path (single main rotor + tail rotor)

Memorize this chain until you can draw it cold:

Engine → drive coupling / short shaft → freewheeling unit → main gearbox (MGB) → mast → main rotor, with the tail rotor driveshaft, oil cooler blower, hydraulic pump, rotor tachometer generator (and on some types a generator and the rotor brake disc) all driven off the main gearbox, not the engine.

That placement is the single most important design fact in this course: everything tapped off the MGB sits downstream of the freewheeling unit. When the engine quits and the freewheel opens, the autorotating main rotor keeps driving the gearbox — so the tail rotor keeps turning (you keep yaw control), the tach keeps reading rotor RPM, and the pumps keep pumping. An autorotation is only survivable because of where those takeoffs are.

  • Drive coupling / short shaft — connects engine output to the gearbox input across small misalignments and structural flexing.
  • Freewheeling unit (FWU) — one-way clutch: engine can drive rotor, rotor can never drive (or drag) a dead engine. Module 2 lives here.
  • Main gearbox — two or three stages of reduction, direction change, and every accessory pad. Module 3.
  • Mast — carries drive torque up to the head and (on most designs) lift loads down into the airframe.
Common student misconception "In autorotation the tail rotor stops because the engine stopped." False — and a student who believes it has not understood the drivetrain at all. Put it on every exam you ever write.
1.3  Piston vs. turbine drivetrains

Piston (R22, R44, S300): the engine cannot start while connected to the inertia of a rotor system, so a clutch (belt drive or centrifugal) lets it start unloaded and then pick up the rotor gradually. Piston installations also produce strong torsional pulses, so the belts or couplings double as vibration isolation. RPM is governed at essentially one operating value (correlator/governor).

Free-turbine (Bell 206, AS350, most turbine helicopters): the power turbine is driven by gas flow, not a shaft, from the gas producer — aerodynamically coupled, mechanically separate. At start, the gas producer spools up while the power turbine and rotor sit still; there is nothing to "disconnect," so no clutch is needed. A freewheeling unit is still mandatory for autorotation. Smooth power delivery, but the gearbox sees high input speed (≈6,000+ RPM), so lubrication and gear precision matter more.

Either way, every helicopter has a freewheeling unit. Only shaft-loaded engines (pistons, and rare direct-drive turbines) need a clutch as well.

1.4  Configurations and what they do to the drive system
  • Single main + tail rotor (the default): main rotor torque tries to spin the fuselage the other way; the tail rotor cancels it, burning roughly 5–15% of total power in the hover (~10% is the classic figure). The whole ATA 65 chapter exists to feed it.
  • Tandem (CH-47): two rotors turn opposite directions — torques cancel, no tail rotor. Engines feed a combining gearbox, and synchronizing shafts tie both rotor gearboxes together so the intermeshing blade arcs stay phased and one engine (or both, in autorotation) always drives both rotors. A sync shaft failure is catastrophic — which is a great way to teach why shaft inspections matter.
  • Coaxial (Kamov): two rotors on concentric shafts through one gearbox; torque cancels on the mast itself.
  • Intermeshing (K-MAX): two close-set masts geared rigidly together — phasing is life-or-death, same teaching point as the tandem.
  • Fenestron (many Airbus types): a small many-bladed fan in a duct — same drive path as a tail rotor but higher shaft and fan RPM.
  • NOTAR (MD): a gearbox-driven fan pressurizes the tailboom; slots and a rotating thruster do the anti-torque work. Still a drivetrain — the fan drive is maintained like any tail rotor drive.

Multi-engine types: each engine input has its own freewheeling unit into a combining section of the MGB, so one engine can fail (or be shut down) while the other drives everything.

1.6  Finding your way: the ATA 60-series map

Type manuals organize rotorcraft chapters like this — your course lives in the bold ones:

  • ATA 62 — Main rotor (head, blades) → the rotors course
  • ATA 63 — Main rotor drive: engine-to-MGB shafting, clutch/FWU, main gearbox, mounts, mast, MGB indicating (chip, temp, press)
  • ATA 64 — Tail rotor (the rotor itself) → rotors course
  • ATA 65 — Tail rotor drive: tail driveshafts, hanger bearings, intermediate and tail gearboxes
  • ATA 66 — Folding blades/pylon; 67 — rotors flight control → elsewhere

Teach students to start every job by finding the right chapter — it's a licensing-exam skill and a shop-floor skill in one.

Week 2 · Aug 3 – 9

Module 2 — Clutches, Freewheeling Units & Couplings

The hardware that connects and disconnects — engine start, power delivery, and the autorotation guarantee.

🎓 Why students love (and fail) this module

This is the "aha" week — sprag clutches are genuinely clever and every student wants to hold one. It's also where exams get failed, because the logic of what drives what, in which direction, in which flight condition takes real thought. Get fluent enough to answer the trick questions cold.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Explain why piston helicopters need a clutch and turbines don't — and why every helicopter needs a freewheeling unit.
  • Describe belt drive and centrifugal clutch systems, their engagement sequences, and their inspection points.
  • Explain sprag and roller-ramp freewheeling units at the level of a cutaway drawing.
  • List the failure modes of a FWU (slip under power / fail to overrun) and their consequences.
  • Identify spline, flexible-disc (Thomas), and elastomeric couplings, what each tolerates, and what rejects them.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

2.1  Two different jobs: engaging vs. overrunning

Keep these separate in your head and your students':

  • Clutch — lets the engine start unloaded, then picks up the rotor system gradually. Needed only when the engine is mechanically shaft-connected to the drive (piston engines; rare direct-drive turbines).
  • Freewheeling unit — a one-way device that transmits torque from engine to rotor but automatically releases the moment the rotor tries to turn faster than the engine. Needed on every helicopter, because a failed engine that stayed connected would be a giant brake on the rotor — the end of autorotation.

A Robinson has both (belts + sprag). A Bell 206 has only the freewheel, because its free turbine needs no clutch. That one comparison carries the whole concept.

2.2  Belt drive systems (Robinson R22/R44)

The engine drives a lower sheave; multiple V-belts carry power up to an upper sheave on the gearbox input. At start the belts are slack — the engine spins freely. After start, the pilot flips the clutch switch and an electric clutch actuator slowly increases sheave separation, tensioning the belts and easing the rotor into motion over a number of seconds. The clutch caution light stays on while the actuator runs; pilots are trained on exactly when that light is and isn't acceptable.

Why belts? Soft engagement, cheap overhaul, and superb isolation of piston power pulses from the gearbox. What you inspect and teach:

  • Belt condition (cracking, fraying, glazing) and matched sets — belts are replaced as a set.
  • Sheave alignment and condition — misalignment eats belts.
  • Actuator operation, rigging, and travel limits per the MM.
  • Manufacturer service documents — Robinson issues plenty on this system; finding them is a good student exercise.
2.3  Centrifugal clutches

Used on types like the Schweizer/Hughes 300 lineage: friction shoes on the engine-driven member fly outward with RPM and grip a drum on the driven side. Below engagement RPM the engine idles free; advance the throttle and engagement happens automatically and progressively. Inspection points are what you'd expect from any friction device: lining wear and glazing, drum scoring and cracks, engagement RPM within limits, no oil contamination on the friction surfaces. Slipping past the engagement band shows up as RPM without rotor response and as heat.

2.4  Freewheeling units: sprag and roller-ramp

The dominant design is the sprag clutch: a full circle of hourglass-shaped steel elements ("sprags") between a smooth inner race and smooth outer race, lightly loaded by energizing springs so they always touch both races. The sprag's genius is its geometry — its diagonal dimension is slightly greater than the race gap:

  • Drive direction: relative rotation tips each sprag so it wedges between the races — the harder you drive, the harder it grips. Torque passes through with no slip.
  • Overrun: the moment the driven race (rotor side) runs faster than the driving race (engine side), the sprags tip the other way, un-wedge, and the races slip freely with only light spring drag.

The roller-and-ramp type does the same job with rollers riding ramps: rolled up the ramp = jammed = drive; rolled down = free. Either way the action is automatic, instant, and purely mechanical — no pilot input, ever.

Where it sits varies by type — at the engine output (Bell 206), or built into the gearbox input — but it is always between the engine and the point where the tail rotor drive and accessories tap off. Check the type MM and make your students find it in the IPC.

Five-minute demo that lands every time A bicycle rear wheel is a roller/pawl freewheel: pedal = drive, coast = overrun, and the clicking is the elements skipping. Bring one, or pass a real sprag unit around. Once they've coasted a bicycle, they understand autorotation drive continuity forever.
2.5  FWU failure modes, checks, and why we care

Two ways it can fail, both taught as consequences:

  • Slips in the drive direction (worn/rounded sprags, wrong or contaminated lube, brinelled races): engine RPM rises but rotor RPM droops under load — power isn't arriving. Shows up as unexplained needle split under power, heat, and metal in the lube.
  • Fails to overrun (jammed, corroded, damaged): the rotor stays chained to a dead or seized engine. In an engine failure this steals the autorotation; on shutdown it shows up as the rotor dragging the dead engine around. This is why overrun checks are built into normal ops — e.g., watching the needles split cleanly (rotor RPM above engine RPM) at throttle chop / shutdown.

Maintenance items: correct lubrication (many FWUs are fed engine or gearbox oil — type-specific), smooth overrun by hand where the MM allows, conditional inspection after any overspeed or sudden stoppage, and scrupulous cleanliness during assembly — a sprag cage assembled dry or dirty is a time bomb.

2.6  Driveshafts and couplings, engine to gearbox

Engine and gearbox are mounted on different structure that flexes, grows with heat, and vibrates — so the short shaft between them must transmit full torque while forgiving small angular and axial misalignment. Three families:

  • Splined couplings — simple and compact; need the right grease, wear limits on the splines, and honest alignment. Fretting and spline wear are the enemies.
  • Flexible-disc packs ("Thomas couplings") — stacks of thin stainless discs bolted alternately to driving and driven flanges; they transmit torque in tension and flex to absorb misalignment. Inspect for cracks radiating from bolt holes, fretting between discs, and correct bolt torque/orientation — and treat them as precision parts: a dropped or bent disc pack is rejected, not straightened.
  • Elastomeric/flexible-frame couplings (e.g., KAflex-style shafts) — flex elements do the same job with no lubrication; inspect per the MM and respect handling limits (they hate being used as a step or pry point).

Teach the pattern once and it covers Module 5 too, because the tail rotor driveline uses the same coupling families end to end.

Week 3 · Aug 10 – 16

Module 3 — Main Gearbox, Mounts & Mast

The heart of the machine: where 6,000 RPM becomes 400, and where the whole aircraft literally hangs from the rotor.

🎓 The centre of your course

Every other module feeds this one. If you can stand at a cutaway gearbox and narrate the path of power through each stage — and the path of lift down through the case into the airframe — you own the course. That second path surprises students every year: on most helicopters, the fuselage hangs off the transmission.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Name the gear types used in each stage of a typical MGB and justify why each was chosen.
  • Compute a planetary-stage reduction ratio from tooth counts.
  • Explain "transmission-limited," torque indication, and what an overtorque means for maintenance.
  • Describe mounting arrangements — rigid, elastomeric, lift link, nodal beam — and what loads each element carries.
  • Contrast a conventional (live) mast with a static mast design.
  • Explain gear pattern and backlash checks, spline wear, and why dynamic components carry finite lives.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

3.1  What the main gearbox actually does (four jobs)
  1. Reduce speed — two or three stages taking ~6,000+ RPM down to a few hundred.
  2. Change direction — engine shafts are roughly horizontal, the mast is vertical; a bevel stage turns the corner.
  3. Distribute power — tail rotor takeoff, oil pumps, hydraulic pump, tach generator, cooler blower, rotor brake.
  4. React loads — rotor thrust (lift!), torque reaction, and gyroscopic/maneuvering loads all pass through the case and mounts into the airframe.

Job 4 is the one students miss. On a typical design the lift developed by the rotor comes down the mast, into the gearbox case, and into the fuselage through the mounts and lift link — the gearbox case is primary structure. That's why case cracks, mount condition, and hardware torque are treated with flight-control-level seriousness.

3.2  Gear stages and why each type is used
  • Spiral bevel — first stage on most boxes: turns the drive through ~90° and takes the first bite of reduction. Curved teeth give multiple teeth in contact = quiet and strong, but they generate thrust loads, so they ride on serious bearings and their mesh position is adjusted by shimming (see pattern checks, 3.6).
  • Spur / helical — used for accessory drives and intermediate stages. Spur = simple, no axial thrust, noisier. Helical = smoother and stronger for the size, but makes axial thrust.
  • Planetary (epicyclic) — the classic final stage: a central sun gear (input) drives several planet gears on a carrier inside a fixed, internally-toothed ring gear; the carrier is the output. Load splits across all the planet meshes at once, everything stays concentric with the mast, and you get a big reduction in a short, fat, light package — exactly what you want directly under a rotor.

The ratio formula (fixed ring, sun in, carrier out): ratio = 1 + (ring teeth ÷ sun teeth). Example: ring 120t, sun 40t → 1 + 3 = 4:1. Have students compute this from a real cutaway — counting teeth on a training aid is a lab they remember.

Big machines stack two planetary stages, or use split-torque layouts; the principle doesn't change.

3.3  Torque limits and "transmission-limited"

On many helicopters the engine can produce more power than the gearbox is rated to carry — especially at low density altitude. The aircraft is then transmission-limited: the torque gauge, not the engine gauges, is the limit the pilot flies to. The torquemeter is effectively a gearbox protection instrument.

Maintenance consequences you'll teach:

  • An overtorque is an exceedance event: the MM prescribes a conditional inspection scaled to how far and how long the limit was exceeded — from a close visual and chip check up to gearbox removal and teardown. Honest reporting culture matters; an unreported overtorque flies around as hidden fatigue damage.
  • Torque limits can differ for single-engine vs. twin operation, takeoff vs. continuous — the MM/RFM tables rule.
3.4  Cases, hardware, and the magnesium problem

Gearbox cases are typically magnesium or aluminum castings — light, stiff, good heat paths. Magnesium buys the most weight and costs the most vigilance: it corrodes enthusiastically, especially where protective finish is chipped, under steel fasteners, and around dissimilar-metal joints. Teach students to treat any blistered paint or white/grey powder on a case as a finding, and to repair finish per the MM (not with whatever primer is on the shelf).

  • Threads live in steel inserts/studs — the casting itself never takes wrench loads directly. Pulled studs and insert condition are inspection items.
  • Cases carry sight glasses, chip detector bosses, filler and drain points, and mounting pads — all leak-path and crack-inspection territory.
  • Lifting and handling per the MM only — a gearbox slung from the wrong point can be damaged invisibly.
3.5  Mounting systems: how the aircraft hangs on

The mounts must carry lift and torque reaction into the airframe while keeping rotor vibration out of the cabin. Designs you should be able to sketch:

  • Rigid pad mounts — simple, all loads straight into structure; vibration comes along too.
  • Elastomeric isolation mounts — rubber/metal sandwich mounts tuned to absorb the rotor's dominant frequencies. Inspect for separation, cracking, set (sag), and contamination by oil.
  • Lift link / lift beam — a dedicated tension member carrying the main thrust load from the transmission to a strong fuselage frame; the surrounding mounts then mostly handle torque and shear. A cracked or worn lift link is a grounding find — make students say why.
  • Nodal beam suspension (Bell's "Noda-Matic") — the transmission sits on a beam engineered to flex so its node points (points of minimum motion) are where the fuselage attaches: the beam vibrates, the cabin doesn't. Beautiful physics teaching moment.
  • Torque restraints / stops — limit transmission rotation under drive torque, and often limit motion if an elastomeric element fails.

Inspection themes: hardware torque and safetying, elastomer condition, fretting at fittings, cracks at the transmission deck, and correct free play (some designs deliberately have motion — "pylon rock" — with limits).

3.6  Masts, bearings, and the checks that verify a healthy box

Conventional (live) mast: the rotating mast carries drive torque up to the head and rotor thrust down into the gearbox — the mast bearing set feeds lift into the case. Mast, mast nut, and mast bearings are critical parts with finite lives; the mast nut on many types is a torque-and-witness-mark item students must learn to respect.

Static mast (MD 500 family and others): a stationary structural tube carries all the flight loads to the airframe, while a drive shaft inside it carries only torque to the head. Elegant separation of jobs — the drive shaft can be light because it never sees lift. Use the contrast as an exam question: "What loads does the drive shaft carry in a static-mast design?"

Verifying gear health without opening the box:

  • Backlash — measured free play between meshing teeth; outside limits means wear or wrong shimming.
  • Tooth contact pattern — marking compound on the teeth, roll the mesh, read where the contact footprint sits; shims move the pattern. Set at build/overhaul, checked when the MM demands.
  • Spline wear — measured against limits; fretting powder (red/black dust) at a spline is an early confession.
  • Chip detectors, oil analysis, HUMS — the everyday windows into the box; all of Module 4.

Lives: gears, masts, and couplings are typically retirement-lifed (fatigue) and/or given TBOs; records make the part airworthy as much as its condition does. That thought carries straight into Module 7.

Week 4 · Aug 17 – 23

Module 4 — Lubrication & Health Monitoring

Gearboxes rarely fail without whispering first. This module is about hearing the whisper: oil, chips, analysis, and HUMS.

🎓 The AME's daily reality

Of everything in this course, this is what your graduates will do most often: service gearboxes, pull and read chip detectors, take oil samples, and decide — or escalate — what a finding means. It's also where your own fixed-wing experience transfers most directly; what's new is how much trust rotorcraft place in these systems, because a main gearbox has no redundancy.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Explain what gearbox oil must do and why oil types are never interchangeable.
  • Draw a typical pressure lubrication system: pump, relief, filter with bypass, jets, cooler with thermostatic bypass, indications.
  • Describe loss-of-lubrication certification logic and what "run-dry" capability means.
  • Run the chip-light decision process and interpret debris like a tradesperson.
  • Read an oil analysis (SOAP) trend and say which metals point where.
  • Explain what HUMS measures, what it catches, and what it has missed.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

4.1  What the oil must do — and why types never mix

Gearbox oil has four jobs: maintain a load-carrying film at extreme tooth contact pressures, cool (a big MGB turns real kilowatts into heat), carry debris to the filter and chip detectors, and protect against corrosion. Depending on the type, the approved fluid may be a turbine-type synthetic or a dedicated EP (extreme-pressure) gear oil — and the additive chemistries are not compatible. Servicing with the wrong fluid, or topping up one family with the other, can strip film strength exactly where tooth pressures peak.

  • Only the fluids listed in the MM/type certificate data — no "equivalents" from the shelf.
  • Cleanliness discipline: dedicated containers, no open cans, wipe fill areas. A gearbox swallows whatever the funnel brings.
  • Levels read per the MM (attitude, temperature, settle time). Overfilling causes churning, foaming, heat, and leaks that get misdiagnosed.
4.2  System architectures: splash to full pressure

Splash/wet sump: small boxes — tail rotor and intermediate gearboxes, some light-helicopter MGBs — where the gears fling oil where it's needed. Servicing is a sight glass and a level plug; monitoring is a chip plug and your eyes.

Pressure systems (most turbine MGBs) — be able to draw this chain:

  • Pump (gearbox-driven — so it works in autorotation too) → relief valve setting system pressure.
  • Filter with a bypass valve — a blocking filter must never starve the box, so the bypass opens and unfiltered oil flows; an impending-bypass indicator (pop-out button or light) confesses before that happens. Checking it is a daily-inspection classic.
  • Oil jets aimed precisely at gear meshes and bearings — a blocked jet is a local failure nobody sees until the chips arrive.
  • External cooler (with its gearbox-driven blower) and a thermostatic bypass so cold oil warms up quickly instead of being forced through the cooler.
  • Indications: pressure and temperature gauges plus low-pressure / high-temperature warnings, with RFM limits attached to each.
4.3  Loss of lubrication and "run-dry" capability

A main gearbox has no backup gearbox. Certification (e.g., FAR/CS 29.927 for transport rotorcraft) therefore requires demonstrating continued safe operation for a stated period after loss of primary lubrication — the widely-quoted figure is on the order of 30 minutes at reduced power for Category A machines: enough to fly to a landing, not enough to finish the sector. Design answers include emergency lube sprays, oil-retaining sump geometry, and generous thermal mass.

Teach it as a decision lesson: a gearbox oil pressure warning over inhospitable terrain is a land-now conversation, and the maintenance side of that promise is everything in this module done right. (The H225 North Sea history — Module 7 — is the sobering case-study anchor.)

4.4  Chip detectors and the chip-light decision

Ferrous debris is the gearbox's confession, and magnetic chip detectors are the microphone. Know the family tree:

  • Plain magnetic plugs — pulled and read on inspection; usually seated in self-closing valves so you can remove the plug without draining the box (and yes, students will forget that valve exists — brief them before the lab, or budget for a mop).
  • Indicating detectors — debris bridges a gap, closes a circuit, lights the caution.
  • Pulsed / "fuzz-burn" systems — automatically zap away non-significant fine fuzz; if the debris survives the pulse, it's big enough to mean it, and the light stays on. Cuts nuisance landings without hiding real events.

Reading debris (with the MM's criteria, not folklore):

  • Fine fuzz / hair-like whiskers — normal wear products, assessed by quantity and trend.
  • Flat, shiny platelets — classic bearing spall flakes: a rolling surface is shedding its skin.
  • Chunks, slivers, gear-tooth fragments — active failure; the box comes off.

The chip-light process: in flight it's an RFM matter (typically land as soon as practicable, or worse); on the ground the MM's conditional inspection governs — capture the debris, identify it, inspect per the flowchart, and document. The culture point to hammer in class: "wiped it off and sent it" is how accident reports start. Every chip event gets the MM process.

4.5  Oil analysis and filter debris analysis

Spectrometric oil analysis (SOAP) measures wear metals in parts per million. Single values mean little; trends mean everything — a step change in slope is the alarm. The decoder ring you'll teach:

Element risingUsual source in a gearbox
Iron (Fe)Steel gears, bearings, shafts — general wear or spalling
Copper (Cu)Bronze bearing cages, bushings, thrust washers
Silver (Ag)Silver-plated cages/components (where used)
Magnesium / AluminumCase metal — something is wearing against the housing, or corrosion
Chromium / NickelPlated or alloyed steel components

Pair SOAP with filter debris analysis — cutting open or patch-checking filters catches particles too big for spectrometry (spectrometers see dissolved/fine metal best). The two methods bracket the particle-size spectrum; chip detectors catch the biggest pieces. That "three overlapping nets" idea is a tidy whiteboard diagram.

4.6  HUMS: listening to the gears

Health and Usage Monitoring Systems mount accelerometers on gearbox cases and bearings supports, record vibration continuously, and reduce it to condition indicators (CIs) per component. The physics: every gear mesh produces a tone at tooth-meshing frequency (teeth × shaft speed); damage — a cracked or pitted tooth — modulates that tone once per revolution, creating sidebands and rising CI values long before chips appear. Ground stations trend the data; maintenance reacts to threshold crossings and slope changes.

Widely fitted to medium/heavy offshore fleets (and mandated in that world) after hard-won North Sea experience. Be honest with students about the limits: HUMS has caught many failures early — and famously did not give usable warning of the planet-gear failures behind the 2009 and 2016 Super Puma accidents (Module 7). Monitoring narrows risk; it doesn't repeal it. Chip detectors, oil analysis, HUMS, and disciplined inspection are layers, not alternatives.

Module 5 — Tail Rotor Drive & Rotor Brake

Forty feet of high-speed shafting, two little gearboxes, and zero redundancy — the driveline that keeps the nose pointed forward.

🎓 Long, thin, and unforgiving

The tail rotor drive is mechanically simple and completely intolerant of neglect: thin-wall shafts spinning thousands of RPM, small grease-lubed bearings, couplings flexing with every gust of boom bending — and if any link breaks, the aircraft yaws violently with no backup. Students should leave this module a little bit paranoid, in the professional sense.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Trace a complete tail rotor drive from MGB takeoff to tail rotor hub, naming every element.
  • Explain hanger bearings, flexible couplings, and splined slip joints — what each accommodates and how each fails.
  • Describe intermediate and tail rotor gearbox construction, lubrication, and inspection.
  • State why shaft dents, nicks, and unbalanced reassembly are serious, and outline shaft balancing.
  • Explain rotor brake operation, location logic, application limits, and inspection.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

5.1  The drive path down the boom

From the tail rotor takeoff on the main gearbox (downstream of the freewheel — Module 2's point, now made concrete), a series of driveshaft segments runs aft along the tailboom spine: typically thin-wall aluminum tubes with bonded or riveted end fittings, turning at high speed — order of 2,000–4,000+ RPM depending on type — because transmitting power through a slender shaft is far easier at high speed / low torque (P = τω again: same lesson, opposite direction).

Why segments instead of one long shaft? A long slender shaft would whip ("shaft whirl") at operating speed, and the boom flexes in flight. Short segments supported by bearings, joined by flexible couplings, ride through all of it. Some types instead run one long supercritical shaft with dampers — if yours does, the MM's damper inspections become the teaching point.

5.2  Hanger bearings, couplings, and slip joints
  • Hanger bearings — grease-packed ball bearings in brackets along the boom, holding the shaft line straight. Failure modes: loss of grease, contamination, brinelling, overheat. Inspection: roughness and play by hand, evidence of grease loss, and overheat indications — discoloration or heat-indicating paint/stickers where fitted; several types call for a careful post-run temperature check by feel. A hanger bearing running dry announces itself with heat and a howl before it seizes — teach crews and students to take both seriously.
  • Flexible couplings between segments — the same disc-pack and flex-frame families as Module 2, taking the small angular misalignments as the boom breathes. Inspect discs for cracks from bolt holes, fretting dust (red/black powder = a confession), correct hardware and torque.
  • Splined slip joints — at least one, somewhere: the boom lengthens and shortens with load and temperature, so the shaft line must change length too. Lubed splines, wear limits, correct engagement depth.
Shop habit worth teaching "Walk the boom" after any tail work: run a hand along every coupling and bearing bracket, look for fretting dust, missing hardware, tools left on the shelf brackets. Two minutes; catches careers' worth of trouble.
5.3  Intermediate and tail rotor gearboxes

Intermediate gearbox (IGB) — fitted where the drive must climb the vertical fin at an angle (the classic UH-1 42° box at the fin base). Small spiral-bevel set, usually splash-lubed, with sight glass, vent, chip plug, and seals. Not every type has one — a straight run to a fin-top gearbox may not need it; your shop type's MM settles it.

Tail rotor gearbox (TGB) — the ~90° spiral-bevel box at the tail rotor, usually with the final reduction for the tail rotor's own RPM. Two details students must see:

  • The pitch-change mechanism passes through it — on most types a control shaft runs through the hollow TGB output shaft to work the tail rotor pitch. Drive and control cohabit; maintenance on one risks the other, which is why rigging checks follow TGB work.
  • It's a sealed splash-lubed world of its own: level, leaks, chip plug, mounting hardware, and seal condition are the whole inspection story — small box, short list, zero tolerance.

Fenestron variants drive a many-bladed fan at higher RPM inside a duct — same drive logic, higher speeds, tighter balance. NOTAR replaces the tail rotor with a gearbox-driven variable-pitch fan in the boom root — the "tail rotor drive" course content becomes fan drive content, same inspections in different clothes.

5.4  Shaft care and balance — why a dent is a big deal

A thin-wall tube at thousands of RPM lives on the edge of fatigue. A dent, nick, gouge, or corrosion pit is a stress riser in a part seeing fully-reversed bending every revolution — tens of millions of cycles per hundred flight hours. Hence the MM's blunt damage limits, often effectively "polish out within barely-visible limits or reject." Chafing from clamps and wiring, and paint stripper attack on bonded fittings, are the classic silent killers.

Balance: shaft assemblies are balanced — as segments, sometimes as an installed set, with balance weights or hardware position marked. Swap segments between positions, mix hardware, or install a coupling half rotated from its match-marks, and you've built a vibrator. Teach: match marks are engineering, not decoration. Rebalancing after component replacement follows the MM (and Module 6 shows the how).

5.5  The rotor brake

A disc-and-caliper (occasionally band) brake, almost always acting on a main gearbox shaft — on the rotor side of the freewheeling unit. Location logic makes a lovely exam question: on the rotor side of the FWU the brake can hold the entire rotor and tail rotor drive system; if it gripped the engine side, the freewheel would simply overrun and the rotors would keep turning, unbraked.

  • Why fitted: stop the rotor quickly after shutdown (passengers, deck operations, wind), hold it stopped in gusts. On many types, applying it above a low rotor RPM limit — or with the engine driving — is prohibited and is itself a conditional-inspection event: it can glaze the disc, overheat the caliper area, or torque the drivetrain backwards against everything's design direction.
  • Interlocks: many designs inhibit engine start with the brake on, or warn when it's applied — type-specific, check the MM.
  • Inspection: pad wear, disc scoring/heat discoloration, hydraulic leaks at the hand pump or master cylinder, full release verified (a dragging rotor brake is a heat source riding on your gearbox), and security of everything near it.
5.6  When the tail stops suddenly — strikes and sudden stoppage

A tail rotor blade touching anything — ground, snow bank, wire, hangar door — sends a torque spike back up the entire driveline in milliseconds. The MM's sudden stoppage / sudden acceleration conditional inspection exists for exactly this: expect it to require inspection (often replacement) of couplings and shafts, hanger bearings, TGB/IGB internals or the boxes outright, the MGB tail takeoff, and sometimes mounts — regardless of visible damage, because torsional overload hides inside splines and gear roots.

This is Module 7's conditional-inspection theme wearing its most common face: strikes happen, and the write-up "checked, no damage found, returned to service" without the MM inspection is indefensible. Build a scenario question around it — it may be the most job-relevant exam item you write.

Module 6 — Vibration & Drivetrain Diagnostics

Frequency is the fingerprint. Learn to name a vibration's source from its speed, and troubleshooting becomes arithmetic plus discipline.

🎓 The diagnostic superpower

Helicopters shake — the question is what kind of shake. The single most valuable idea you'll teach this week: every rotating part signs its work at its own frequency. Match the felt/measured frequency to a shaft speed or gear mesh, and the suspect list collapses from "the helicopter" to two or three parts. Your fixed-wing prop balancing instincts transfer here almost unchanged.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Describe vibration by frequency, amplitude (ips), and phase, and sort helicopter vibration into low / medium / high bands.
  • Compute expected frequencies: 1× shaft, 2× shaft, blade-passing, gear mesh.
  • Attribute signatures: imbalance, misalignment, gear damage, bearing distress.
  • Outline a shaft-balancing job with strobe/phototach equipment and a balance chart.
  • Run a disciplined troubleshooting sequence on three classic scenarios.
  • Draw the boundary with main rotor track & balance (the rotors course) and say what ground resonance is when asked.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

6.1  The language: frequency, amplitude, phase — and the three bands

Any vibration is described by frequency (events per second, Hz — who is shaking), amplitude (how hard — aviation balance work usually uses velocity in inches per second, ips), and phase (where in the rotation the heavy point passes the reference — which tells you where to put the fix).

Helicopter people sort by feel into three bands, and the sort itself is diagnostic:

  • Low frequency — main rotor territory: 1/rev (a lane-change lurch or vertical bounce, once per blade revolution — track/balance) and n/rev (blade-passing). A ~400 RPM rotor's 1/rev is under 7 Hz — you feel it as motion, not buzz.
  • Medium frequency — typically tail rotor 1/rev and its multiples: a buzz in the pedals is the classic tell.
  • High frequency — drivetrain: engine and driveshaft speeds (tens of Hz) up to gear-mesh tones (hundreds to thousands of Hz — heard as whine as much as felt). New high-frequency noise = drive system until proven otherwise.

Rules of thumb worth teaching with their limits: for main rotor, vertical tends to track/aerodynamics, lateral tends to balance — a starting hypothesis, not a verdict.

6.2  Drivetrain signatures and the arithmetic

The frequency bestiary, in the order students should check it:

  • Imbalance → 1× shaft speed. A tail rotor driveshaft at 3,000 RPM shakes at 3,000 ÷ 60 = 50 Hz. Amplitude grows with RPM².
  • Misalignment / bent shaft → strong 2× (often with some 1×): the shaft gets bent twice per revolution crossing a misaligned joint.
  • Gear damage → gear-mesh frequency = tooth count × shaft revs/sec, and its sidebands. A 40-tooth gear at 3,000 RPM (50 rev/s) meshes at 2,000 Hz — a whine, not a shake. Damage on one tooth modulates the whine once per rev (HUMS lives on this — Module 4 handshake).
  • Bearing distress → non-synchronous tones — frequencies that are not clean multiples of any shaft speed (they follow rolling-element geometry). Often heard as growl/howl; on the boom, felt as a hot hanger bearing (Module 5 handshake).

Type MMs carry a vibration troubleshooting chart listing each component's frequency at operating RPM. Find yours, photocopy it, and teach students to reach for it first — it turns folklore into lookup.

6.3  Tools and the shaft-balancing workflow

Classic kit (Chadwick-Helmuth 8500/Vibrex lineage, MicroVib II, and modern equivalents): an accelerometer/velocity pickup mounted per the MM, a once-per-rev reference (magnetic pickup, or strobe/phototach flashing on reflective tape), and a readout giving ips and phase (clock angle).

The loop, which is identical in spirit to prop balancing:

  1. Run at the specified RPM; record amplitude and phase.
  2. Enter them on the type's balance chart (polar "move line") — it converts your reading into where to add/remove weight or washers.
  3. Make the change exactly as the MM allows (specified hardware at specified stations — never improvised washers).
  4. Run again; iterate to the limit (typically a few tenths of an ips or better, per the MM).

Emphasize the professional habits: same run conditions each time, one change at a time, log every move — a balance job without notes is a random walk.

6.4  Boundaries: track & balance, and ground resonance

Main rotor track & balance belongs to the rotors course (confirmed in Week 0) — but your students won't respect that boundary, so hold one slide in reserve: same instruments, same logic (amplitude + phase → chart → adjustment), applied to blade tracking, pitch links, and hub weights. Then hand it to your colleague.

Ground resonance will come up the first time you say "vibration": it's a self-amplifying interaction between rotor blade lead-lag motion and the airframe rocking on its landing gear — a rotors/dampers/gear-servicing topic, not a drivetrain fault. Know the two-sentence answer, teach that dampers and strut/tire servicing are the defence, and point to where it's taught in full. Knowing what's not yours is part of owning a course.

6.5  The troubleshooting discipline — three worked scenarios

Method first: (1) characterize — when did it start, what changed, which band, where felt? (2) convert band + location to a suspect list with the MM chart; (3) inspect/measure the suspects; (4) change ONE thing; (5) verify by re-run. Now the scenarios — run them in class exactly like this, reveal by stages:

  • "New buzz in the pedals after a coupling replacement last week." Medium/high frequency, tail region, started after maintenance → prime suspects: that coupling's installation (match marks? hardware stack? torque?) and shaft balance. Finding: coupling installed 180° off its match marks. Lesson: the last person to touch it is suspect #1 — and that person might be you.
  • "High-frequency whine getting louder over two weeks, now a chip light and rising TGB temp." Gear-mesh tone + chips + heat = a gearbox eating itself. Actions: stop operating, debris assessment per MM, likely box removal. Lesson: trend + multiple independent symptoms = believe them; three whispers make a shout.
  • "4–5 Hz vertical bounce, worse in translation; drivetrain just inspected clean." Frequency says main rotor 1/rev, vertical suggests track/aerodynamic → not a drive fault at all; refer to track & balance. Lesson: the frequency fingerprint keeps you from tearing apart a healthy drivetrain — diagnosis by arithmetic, not by anxiety.

Module 7 — Inspections, Airworthiness & Case Studies

Mostly ground you already own as a working AME — re-pointed at dynamic components, plus two accidents every drivetrain course should be built around.

🎓 Your home turf, new terrain

You've lived inspection programs, ADs, and records for years — that experience is the most valuable thing you bring to this classroom, so this module is deliberately lighter on new theory. What changes with dynamic components is the stakes profile: single load paths, fatigue-driven lives, and failure modes that give little external warning. Anchor everything in stories — yours, and the two documented ones below.

By the end of this module you can:

  • Lay out the inspection landscape for dynamic components from daily checks to overhaul and retirement.
  • List the conditional-inspection triggers and what each is protecting against.
  • Explain life limits, TBO, and why paperwork makes a part airworthy — in the Canadian regulatory frame.
  • Teach the human-factors angles specific to drivetrain work.
  • Run a 20-minute case-study discussion on each of the two Super Puma gearbox accidents.

📖 Core knowledge refresher

7.1  The inspection landscape for dynamic components
  • Daily / pre-flight: leaks and levels (sight glasses), chip indications, belt condition (pistons), obvious coupling/bearing distress, rotor brake released, filter bypass buttons. Cheap, fast, catches the loud failures.
  • Scheduled inspections (100-hour/annual equivalents and progressive systems): the detailed looks — coupling disc inspections, hanger bearing checks, mount and elastomer condition, spline lube, pattern/backlash where called, torque checks.
  • Overhaul (TBO): gearboxes and FWUs go to shop at fixed intervals for teardown, inspection, re-shim, and re-life of what's allowed; on-condition programs (supported by monitoring — Module 4) extend or replace some of this on some fleets.
  • Retirement (life limits): masts, gears, couplings and similar parts leave service at their fatigue life regardless of apparent condition — the crack that kills initiates below the surface, where no field inspection reliably sees it. This is the concept students most need to internalize: "it looks perfect" is not an airworthiness argument for a lifed part.
7.2  Conditional inspections — the "something happened" list

Collect these into one table for your students; each trigger exists because torsional or shock overload hides inside splines, gear roots, and races:

  • Sudden stoppage / sudden acceleration — rotor strike, tail strike, seized engine (Module 5's deep dive).
  • Overtorque — graded response per magnitude/duration (Module 3).
  • Overspeed — rotor or drivetrain beyond limits; centrifugal and mesh loading exceedances.
  • Chip event — debris assessment and the MM flowchart (Module 4).
  • Rotor brake misuse — applied above limits or against power (Module 5).
  • Lightning strike — current exiting through the drivetrain can arc across bearing contacts, leaving micro-pitting that fails later; MMs direct specific bearing/gearbox checks.
  • Hard landing — mounts, cases, and shaft alignment join the structural inspection.

The teaching thread: none of these depend on visible damage. The event, honestly reported, is the finding — which makes reporting culture an airworthiness system.

7.3  Lives, records, and the Canadian frame

Map your existing knowledge onto this course in one lecture:

  • CAR 571 (maintenance performance rules, ICAs) and CAR 605 / Standard 625 (maintenance schedules, technical records) apply exactly as you know them — illustrate each with a drivetrain example: recording a chip event, tracking a mast's life, complying with a coupling AD.
  • ADs and service bulletins: drivetrain ADs are common across fleets (couplings, sprag units, gearbox components). A great student exercise: pull the current AD list for the school's type and find every ATA 63/65 entry.
  • Component records: a life-limited dynamic part is airworthy only with its history — hours, cycles, event exposure. No traceability = no life status = unairworthy, whatever it looks like. Tie in bogus/suspect parts: dynamic components are exactly where counterfeit history kills.
  • ICAs: the type's maintenance manual, airworthiness limitations section, and overhaul manuals are the legal backbone of everything in Modules 1–6.
7.4  Human factors, drivetrain edition

Generic HF training is easy to nod through; land it with drivetrain-specific failure paths:

  • Torque and safetying discipline — coupling bolts and gearbox hardware live under reversed loads; "close enough" migrates.
  • FOD in open cases — an open gearbox is a mouth; tool control and covers are non-negotiable.
  • Servicing errors — wrong oil family (Module 4), wrong grease on splines, overfill.
  • Match marks and orientation — balance and phase live in the marks (Module 5).
  • Verification culture — disturbed critical assemblies deserve a second set of eyes and honest duplicate/independent checks where required; interruptions are where steps vanish.

Teach with near-miss stories from your own career where you can — nothing you say all term will be remembered better.

7.5  Case study 1: G-REDL — Aberdeen, 1 April 2009

An AS332L2 Super Puma returning from a North Sea platform lost its main rotor in flight; all 16 aboard died. The AAIB traced the failure to a second-stage planet gear in the MGB epicyclic module, which fractured from a fatigue crack most likely originating at spalling damage. Days earlier a magnetic particle had been found during maintenance — and, through a chain of communication and procedure gaps, its significance was missed and the box stayed in service.

Why it belongs in your course: it connects Module 3 (epicyclic gears), Module 4 (what a particle means and what happens when the process around it fails), and this module (culture and process ARE airworthiness). Read the AAIB report's summary and conclusions (AAIB 2/2011 — free online) and build your discussion around one question: "At which points could this chain have been broken — and by whom?"

7.6  Case study 2: LN-OJF — Turøy, 29 April 2016 — and how to run a case class

Seven years later, an H225 Super Puma near Bergen, Norway, also lost its main rotor: 13 dead. Investigation (AIBN/NSIA) again found a second-stage planet gear fatigue failure — the crack initiating below the surface from spalling-type damage, propagating with little detectable warning: the debris shed was limited and HUMS gave no actionable alert. The aftermath reshaped European rules on gearbox monitoring, debris analysis, and design assumptions.

The paired lesson of the two accidents is honest and uncomfortable: chip detection, oil analysis, and HUMS are overlapping nets with real holes; that's exactly why conservative lives, conditional inspections, honest reporting, and process discipline carry so much of the load. Students who understand this stop seeing paperwork as bureaucracy.

Running a 20-minute case discussion: (1) five minutes of facts — no blame language; (2) small groups map the failure chain onto the systems they've learned (which module would have touched it?); (3) collect "break points" on the whiteboard; (4) close with what changed after — and what a technician on the floor controls personally. Never run it as "look how they screwed up" — run it as "this is why your signature matters."

8.1  Backward design in one paragraph

8.3  The 50-minute lesson plan template

One page per session, six lines — write these and you will never stand in front of a class wondering what's next:

  • Hook (5 min): a question, story, or object. ("Who votes we bolt the rotor straight to the engine?")
  • Outcome stated (1 min): "By the end you'll be able to…" — say it out loud; students aim better when they can see the target.
  • Present (15–20 min): the content, with the visual you built. Max 20 minutes before they do something.
  • Guided practice (15 min): worksheet, calculation, hardware handling, scenario — the deliverables you've filed.
  • Check (5 min): 3–5 rapid questions (your quiz bank), cold-called kindly.
  • Summarize + bridge (3 min): one-sentence recap; one-sentence teaser for next session.

Write the Module 2 sprag lesson first as your worked example — it has the best hook, demo, and questions. Then batch the rest; after three they take 20 minutes each.

8.4  Assessment without misery
  • Blueprint before writing: decide marks per module proportional to teaching hours and importance (e.g., FWU and MGB heavy; configurations light). It keeps the exam honest and defensible.
  • Question craft: stems that pose a complete problem; one defensibly correct answer; distractors built from real student misconceptions ("the tail rotor stops in autorotation") — you've been collecting them all summer in these quizzes.
  • Practical assessment: a simple observed-task sheet per lab (task, steps observed, pass criteria, retry policy). Inspecting a coupling to MM limits IS an exam.
  • Early diagnostic: a no-stakes 10-question quiz in week one tells you what the class already knows and buys you calibration time.
8.5  First day, and surviving the first month
  • First day: the power-path hook, the course map ("seven systems, one promise: power gets to the rotors every single flight"), expectations and marking scheme, then teach real content for at least 20 minutes — day one sets the tone that class time means learning.
  • The parking lot: a whiteboard corner for questions you can't answer on the spot. Say "I'll find out" without shame, come back next session with the answer and the reference. Students trust an instructor who models looking things up over one who bluffs — and it demonstrates the exact skill their licence depends on.
  • Stay one week ahead, not one term: before each week, re-skim the matching module here and your filed deliverables. Your prep is done; trust it.
  • Backup plans: for every lab, know what you'll do if the hardware/aircraft is unavailable (photos, video, worksheet version). For every lecture, know your board-only version if the projector dies.

Glossary

The working vocabulary of the course. If you can define these cold, you can survive any student question sprung mid-sentence.

Autorotation
Power-off flight in which upflow through the rotor keeps it turning. The drivetrain's job: the freewheeling unit releases the engine while the main rotor keeps driving the gearbox, tail rotor, and accessories.
ATA 63 / ATA 65
The maintenance-manual chapters for main rotor drive (63) and tail rotor drive (65) — where this whole course lives. ATA 62/64 are the rotors themselves.
Backlash
Measured free play between meshing gear teeth. Outside limits it indicates wear or incorrect shimming; checked with the tooth contact pattern.
Chip detector
Magnetic plug (plain, indicating, or pulsed/fuzz-burning) that captures ferrous debris from gearbox oil — the everyday early-warning window into a gearbox.
Centrifugal clutch
RPM-operated friction clutch: shoes fly outward with engine speed and grip a drum, engaging the rotor progressively and automatically.
Combining gearbox
Gearbox joining the power of two or more engines into a common drive — each input protected by its own freewheeling unit.
Condition indicator (CI)
A HUMS-computed metric trending the vibration health of one component (a gear mesh, a bearing). Threshold crossings and slope changes drive maintenance action.
Conditional inspection
An inspection triggered by an event — sudden stoppage, overtorque, overspeed, chip light, lightning, rotor brake misuse — required regardless of visible damage.
Duplex / stacked bearings
Paired bearings sharing thrust loads, common at mast and bevel-gear positions where lift and gear thrust concentrate.
Epicyclic (planetary) stage
Sun gear driving planet gears on a carrier inside a fixed ring gear. Shares load across several meshes; the classic compact final reduction under the rotor. Ratio (fixed ring) = 1 + ring/sun teeth.
Flexible disc coupling (Thomas coupling)
Stacked thin stainless discs bolted alternately to driving and driven flanges; transmits torque in tension while flexing for misalignment. Rejected for cracks at bolt holes, fretting, or handling damage.
Freewheeling unit (FWU)
One-way clutch (sprag or roller-ramp) between engine and gearbox: drives normally, overruns instantly when the rotor outruns the engine. The hardware that makes autorotation possible.
Fretting
Micro-motion wear between clamped surfaces (splines, coupling discs, fittings), confessing as fine red/black oxide dust — an early sign of looseness or wear.
Fuzz burner (pulsed chip detector)
Chip detector that electrically clears insignificant fine fuzz; debris that survives the pulse is significant, so the warning means business.
Gear mesh frequency
Tooth count × shaft revolutions per second — the tone every gear pair sings at. Damage modulates it, creating sidebands.
Ground resonance
Self-amplifying interaction between rotor lead-lag motion and the airframe on its landing gear. A rotors/dampers topic — know the boundary answer.
Hanger bearing
Grease-packed support bearing holding tail rotor driveshaft segments in line along the boom. Fails hot, rough, and loud — checked by hand, eye, and heat indication.
HUMS
Health and Usage Monitoring System: accelerometers plus recorders trending drivetrain vibration into condition indicators. Powerful early warning; not infallible.
Impending-bypass indicator
Pop-out button or light warning that the oil filter is loading toward bypass — a confession to investigate, never just reset.
Intermediate gearbox (IGB)
Small bevel gearbox changing driveline direction where the shaft climbs the fin (e.g., the UH-1's 42° box). Splash-lubed, sight glass, chip plug.
ips
Inches per second — velocity amplitude of vibration; the working unit of balance jobs.
KAflex shaft
A flexible-frame driveshaft design using flexing elements instead of discs or splines — no lubrication, strict handling rules.
Life-limited (retirement-lifed) part
Component retired at a set fatigue life regardless of condition — masts, gears, couplings. The records carry the life; no traceability, no airworthiness.
Lift link / lift beam
Dedicated structural member carrying main rotor thrust from the transmission into a fuselage frame. A cracked lift link is a grounding find.
Match marks
Alignment marks preserving the balanced/phased relationship of shaft and coupling components. Engineering, not decoration.
Mast (live)
Rotating shaft carrying drive torque up to the rotor head and (in conventional designs) rotor thrust down into the gearbox.
Static mast
Non-rotating structural tube carrying flight loads to the airframe while an internal shaft carries torque only (MD 500 family).
Nodal beam (Noda-Matic)
Bell transmission suspension tuned so the fuselage attaches at the beam's vibration nodes — the beam shakes, the cabin doesn't.
Overrunning
The freewheeling unit's released state: rotor-side race turning faster than engine-side race, sprags un-wedged, no torque transfer.
Overtorque
Exceedance of a drive-system torque limit — a maintenance event with a conditional inspection graded by magnitude and duration.
Phase (balance)
The angular position where the heavy spot passes the once-per-rev reference — tells you where the correction goes.
Pylon
The transmission support structure/area; "pylon rock" describes designed motion of an isolated transmission, with limits.
Rotor brake
Disc/caliper brake on the rotor side of the freewheel that stops and holds the rotor system after shutdown. RPM application limits; misuse is a conditional-inspection event.
Run-dry capability
Demonstrated continued gearbox operation for a certification period (order of 30 minutes) after loss of primary lubrication.
Sidebands
Frequencies flanking a gear-mesh tone created when damage modulates the mesh once per revolution — HUMS's favourite clue.
Sight glass
Window for oil level/condition on gearboxes — first item of every daily inspection.
Slip joint (splined)
Axially sliding splined joint letting the tail driveline change length as the boom flexes and breathes with temperature.
SOAP
Spectrometric Oil Analysis Program: wear metals in ppm, read as trends. Fe = steel, Cu = bronze cages/bushings, Mg/Al = case.
Spalling
Fatigue of a loaded rolling/gear surface shedding its hardened skin as flakes — the classic origin story of bearing and gear failures (and of both Super Puma accidents).
Spiral bevel gears
Curved-tooth bevel set turning drive ~90° quietly and strongly; generates thrust loads and is positioned by shimming, verified by pattern checks.
Sprag clutch
Freewheeling unit using hourglass-shaped elements that wedge between races in the drive direction and tip free in overrun.
Sudden stoppage
Abrupt arrest/shock of the drive system (blade strike, seizure). Triggers a deep conditional inspection of the whole driveline regardless of visible damage.
Tail rotor gearbox (TGB)
The ~90° bevel box at the tail rotor with the final reduction; typically splash-lubed with the pitch-change shaft passing through its hollow output.
TBO
Time Between Overhauls — the scheduled teardown/refurbishment interval for gearboxes and dynamic components.
Tooth contact pattern
Marking-compound footprint showing where gear teeth actually touch; moved by shimming, it verifies correct mesh position.
Torquemeter / transmission-limited
The torque indication protecting the gearbox where the engine can out-deliver it — the limit the pilot actually flies to.
Wet sump (splash) lubrication
Gears flinging oil from an internal sump — the lubrication scheme of small gearboxes (TGB/IGB and some light MGBs).

Resources

The library for the eight weeks. Free and official first; books worth buying second; type documents from your school always.

🆓 Free and official

  • FAA Aviation Maintenance Technician Handbooks (General, Airframe, Powerplant) — the Airframe volume's rotorcraft chapter covers transmissions, FWUs, and tail drives at exactly course depth. Free PDFs: faa.gov → Handbooks & Manuals → Aviation.
  • FAA Helicopter Flying Handbook (FAA-H-8083-21) — chapters on rotor systems, autorotation, and helicopter systems give you the operational "why" behind every component you'll teach. Same FAA handbooks page.
  • Type-certificate holders' public safety pages — Robinson's published safety notices and service documents are teaching gold for belt drives and sprag clutches (robinsonheli.com); Bell and Airbus publish service/safety information portals your school likely has access to.
  • AAIB report 2/2011 (G-REDL) — search "AAIB G-REDL" at gov.uk. Read synopsis, gearbox findings, conclusions.
  • NSIA (formerly AIBN) report on LN-OJF — search "LN-OJF report" at nsia.no. Same sections.
  • Transport Canada CARs — CAR 571, 605, Standard 625, and Standard 566 for the licensing/training frame: tc.canada.ca → Canadian Aviation Regulations.

📕 Worth buying (or borrowing from the program)

  • “Helicopter Maintenance” — Joseph Schafer (Jeppesen). The classic AMT-school helicopter text; its powertrain, transmission, and tail rotor drive chapters map almost one-to-one onto Modules 1–6. If you buy one book, buy this.
  • “Principles of Helicopter Flight” — W.J. Wagtendonk. For the aerodynamic side (autorotation, tip speed, power required) — keeps your "why" explanations honest.
  • Your school's library may already hold both, plus older gems (e.g., military rotorcraft maintenance manuals) that make excellent lab handout material.

📘 Type documents (from the school)

  • Maintenance manuals, ATA 63 and 65, for every type in the training fleet — your primary source all summer. Descriptions first, then inspection sections, then conditional inspections.
  • Illustrated parts catalogs — the cutaway views are ready-made worksheet art, and IPC navigation is itself a course skill.
  • Rotorcraft flight manuals — limits sections (torque, rotor brake RPM, chip light procedures) connect your systems teaching to what pilots are told.
  • The AD list for each training type — filter for ATA 63/65 items for Module 7's exercise.
