The Three Things You Actually Need

Everything in electronics comes back to three quantities and one law. Get comfortable with these and the rest is just detail.

Voltage, Current & Resistance — the water analogy

Picture water in a pipe. It's the single best mental model for electricity, and it holds up surprisingly far.

  • Voltage (V) — the pressure pushing the water. Measured in volts. A dead battery has low pressure; the mains wall socket has a lot.
  • Current (I) — how much water actually flows. Measured in amps. This is what does the work — and what burns things out when it's too high.
  • Resistance (R) — how much the pipe fights the flow. Measured in ohms (Ω). A kinked hose is high resistance; a wide open pipe is low.
The key insight for troubleshooting: a short circuit is a pipe with almost no resistance — huge current, blows fuses. An open circuit is a pipe cut in half — no current, nothing works. Nearly every fault you'll chase is one of those two.

Ohm's Law — play with it

The whole thing is one equation: V = I × R. Voltage equals current times resistance. Drag the sliders and watch how they push on each other.

Reading it: raise the voltage and current rises. Raise the resistance and current falls. That trade-off is what you're doing every time you probe a circuit and ask "does this reading make sense?"

What a "circuit" really is

Current only flows in a complete loop: out of the source's positive side, through the load, and back to the negative side. Break the loop anywhere — a blown fuse, a cracked solder joint, a corroded connector — and everything downstream goes dead.

Ground is just the common "return" side that everything shares — the big copper areas on a board, or the metal chassis. When you measure voltage, you're really measuring the pressure difference between a point and ground. That's why your black meter probe almost always clips to ground and stays there.

This is the foundation of board troubleshooting: black probe on ground, red probe walking along the circuit, asking at each stage — "is the voltage that should be here actually here?" The first place it isn't is at or just before your fault.

How a TV Power Board Works

Almost every modern TV, monitor and charger uses the same trick: a switch-mode power supply (SMPS). Once you understand this signal path, you understand 80% of the boards you'll ever open. Follow the power left to right.

AC mains→ Fuse→ Bridge rectifier→ Big filter cap (~160V DC)→ MOSFET switcher→ Transformer→ Secondary rectifier + caps→ DC rails out

Hit "Animate" to watch power travel from the wall to the DC rails that run the TV.

What each stage does (and where it goes wrong)

  • Fuse — the sacrificial link. Blows to protect everything else. A blown fuse is a symptom, not usually the root cause — something downstream shorted.
  • Bridge rectifier — turns AC (which swings +/−) into pulsing DC (always one direction). Four diodes in a box.
  • Big filter cap — smooths that pulsing DC into a steady ~160 V. This is the fat cylinder that holds a dangerous charge even unplugged.
  • MOSFET switcher — chops the 160 V on and off tens of thousands of times a second. This is the "switch" in switch-mode. Runs hot, fails often.
  • Transformer — steps that chopped voltage down and isolates the dangerous mains side from the safe low-voltage side.
  • Secondary rectifier + caps — convert the transformer's output back to clean, low DC (5 V standby, 12 V, 24 V for the panel, etc.).
  • Standby rail — a small supply (often 5 V) that's always alive so the TV can hear the remote. If standby is dead, the TV shows zero signs of life.
The mental hook: a TV that's totally dead usually has a problem on the primary (mains) side — fuse, bridge, or standby. A TV whose standby light is on but won't fully start usually has a problem on the secondary side — a main rail cap or regulator.

Safety First — Read This Before You Open Anything

This isn't boilerplate. Power boards run on mains voltage and store lethal charge. Respect it and you'll be fine for a lifetime of tinkering.

Mains voltage can kill. The primary side of a TV power board carries ~120 V (or 240 V) AC and ~160 V DC. Never probe a live board with wet hands, on a metal bench, or while distracted. Keep one hand behind your back when probing live so current can't cross your chest.
Big capacitors bite even when unplugged. That fat filter cap can hold 160 V+ for minutes to hours after you pull the plug. Before touching the primary side, discharge it: with the board unplugged, bridge the cap's two legs through a resistor (a ~2W, 1–10kΩ resistor on insulated leads, or a purpose-made discharge tool). Then confirm it reads near 0 V on your meter before proceeding. Never just short it with a screwdriver — that's a spark, pitted metal, and a damaged board.
The safe habits that make it routine:
  • Unplug first. Do visual inspection and continuity/resistance tests with no power whenever you can.
  • Only apply power for the voltage tests that genuinely need it — and know where your probes are before you flip it on.
  • Work on a non-conductive surface, good light, nothing to knock over.
  • Discharge the big cap and verify 0 V before your fingers go near the primary side.
  • When in doubt about a mains-side repair, a bench isolation transformer is cheap insurance.

Most of what you'll actually do — spotting bulging caps, checking the fuse, testing the bridge with the diode function — happens with the board unplugged and discharged. The dangerous live-voltage tests are a small, deliberate part of the job.
